Part 1: Data Part 3: Prototype

October 11th, 2020

In part two of this project, we were tasked with developing a raw HTML structure for organizing our data. The purpose was not only to assign our data a place on a webpage, but also to harness the power of metatags to enhance the usefulness of our page’s information. Luckily for me, I already had some experience with HTML/CSS and knew of a web-based scripting engine called codepen.io. (I had become familiar with this site through my work as a technologist and web designer.)

Due to my previous moves, I can personally attest to the relief well-organized real estate data can bring. Moving is already stressful, with questions, uncertainty and anxiety in abundance. So, I felt it was necessary to lay out information simply and efficiently from the beginning. My submission for this section of the project displayed three sample listings from my data, with the price, street address, and number of bedrooms all available upon login. Links at the top of the screen brought users to each individual listing, while links at the bottom of the screen brought users back to the main navigation menu. You can see my HTML codes and its resulting preview page in the pictures below.

In retrospect, though this website did meet my desire for simplicity and ease of use, it wasn’t particularly layered in structure. Though this outline could be suitable for an organization with just a handful of listings to choose from, for any service with a larger number of sources, it would have been overwhelming and inadequate. (Imagine all 180 of my listings on just one web page; navigation would not have been very user-friendly.)

Some problems I ran into usually had to do with misremembering HTML syntax and accompanying CSS styling choices; how does one center a “nav” menu? How do I import a Google Font using a link? How do I ensure that this paragraph element stays centered no matter its context or screen? A quick look at some online tutorials, such as on w3 Schools or Stack Overflow, provided answers. While I am clearly growing in my web design capabilities, I still have a lot to learn. I look forward to doing so in the future.
